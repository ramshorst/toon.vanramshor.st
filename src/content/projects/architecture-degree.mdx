## Context

Between 2008 and 2013 I studied architecture at the École Nationale Supérieure d'Architecture de Lyon, completing both a bachelor and a master's degree. Architecture was a full training in designing complex systems under real-world constraints, not just an aesthetic discipline.

## What I learned

Architectural education trains you to hold a large number of variables at once: site, programme, structure, regulation, budget, and the expectations of many different stakeholders. You learn to coordinate across professions — engineers, planners, clients, contractors — and to keep that process coherent while making design decisions at every scale.

The design process itself is taught as branching reasoning: exploring multiple directions simultaneously, committing progressively, and being able to articulate and defend choices at each stage. That mode of thinking — systematic but creative, structured but open — translates directly into product and interaction design.

## Why it still matters

I keep a strong interest in architecture and interior design. But more practically, the training left me with a way of approaching complex problems: reading context carefully, understanding how people move through and experience space, reasoning across disciplines, and designing things that have to work for many different people at the same time. That is still how I approach design work today.
