In the 2000s, most websites credited a **webmaster** in the footer. That one name stood for everything:

- Page layout and visual design  
- HTML and CSS (and whatever JavaScript was fashionable)  
- Content creation  
- Hosting, deployment, and support  

The “webmaster” owned the whole experience—from how it looked to how it worked and how fast it loaded over dial-up.

Over the years, that single role fragmented into a parade of specialisations. But in **2025**, with AI-driven “vibe coding” and modern tools, we’re seeing the rise of a new, similarly powerful figure:

> The **designer–engineer hybrid**.

Not a pure designer. Not a pure engineer. Someone who can think in terms of interaction, aesthetics, and user journeys, *and* express those ideas directly in working code.

---

## How We Lost the Full-Stack Designer

As web apps became more complex, the industry optimised for scale:

- Design tools moved from Photoshop to Sketch to Figma.
- Engineering moved from PHP templates to SPAs to micro-frontends.
- Infrastructure moved from shared hosting to containers and serverless.

Handoffs proliferated:

1. Designers produced static mockups.
2. Engineers translated those mockups into React, Angular, or Vue.
3. Product and stakeholders reviewed the implementation and asked for tweaks.
4. The cycle repeated.

Somewhere in this loop, designers lost direct access to the production medium. They drew **representations** of the interface instead of working in the actual material of the web: HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

The result:

- Designers felt their intent diluted.  
- Engineers became gatekeepers of what could or couldn’t be built “within the sprint”.  
- Tiny changes often required a full pass through the pipeline.

---

## 2025: AI and Vibe Coding Change the Equation

With AI-assisted workflows, “vibe coding” has become a real thing:

- You describe the interaction you want.
- You paste a rough layout or component structure.
- The model generates a first pass in your framework of choice.

This dramatically lowers the activation energy for **designers who code a bit** and **engineers who care about design**.

Instead of:

> “I need a frontend engineer to translate this Figma file.”

You get:

> “I can ask my tools to generate a component close to my design and then tweak it myself.”

The designer–engineer hybrid can:

- Iterate on real components instead of static mockups.  
- Validate interactions and motion directly in the browser.  
- Ship prototypes that behave like the final product.  

The pipeline collapses. The number of handoffs shrinks. And the person with the clearest vision for the experience can move the fastest.

## Why Svelte and htmx Fit This New Role

Tools matter a lot for this hybrid role. Two that fit the mental model extremely well are **Svelte** and **htmx**.

### Svelte: Design-Friendly Components

Svelte feels very close to the web’s native materials:

- **Markup first.** You write HTML-like templates with minimal ceremony.
- **Scoped styles.** CSS lives next to the component it belongs to.
- **Reactivity without ceremony.** State updates automatically propagate without a pile of hooks or classes.

For a designer who codes, or an engineer with strong design instincts, this is huge:

- The distance between layout, interaction, and styling is tiny.
- You can open a single `.svelte` file and see the whole picture.
- It doesn’t feel like wrestling a giant framework; it feels like enhanced HTML/CSS/JS.

> I dig into this more here: [Why Svelte Feels Like Real Web Development](/svelte)

### htmx: Hypermedia With Superpowers

htmx goes even further towards “design in HTML”:

- You declare dynamic behaviour directly in your HTML via attributes (`hx-get`, `hx-post`, `hx-swap`, etc.).
- It leans heavily on server-rendered HTML and progressive enhancement.
- You can build rich interactions without a heavy frontend framework.

For the designer–engineer hybrid, htmx means:

- You can prototype interactions by editing templates, not wiring up a full SPA.
- You keep the mental model close to the browser’s: requests, responses, HTML updates.
- You can stay in your comfort zone—typography, layout, flow—while still shipping dynamic features.

Both Svelte and htmx **lower the complexity tax** on expressing a design idea in production-grade code.

## More Control Over Complexity (and Bundle Size)

One of the subtle superpowers of this hybrid role is **control over complexity**.

We’ve normalised:
- Multi-megabyte JavaScript bundles.
- Deep npm dependency trees for even simple components.
- Build systems that need their own architecture diagrams.

The designer–engineer hybrid, equipped with AI, Svelte, and/or htmx, can actively push back:

- Choose approaches that rely on server-rendered HTML where possible.
- Reach for minimal components instead of entire UI libraries “just in case”.
- Use AI to propose **simpler** implementations, not just more layers.

You still end up in npm hell sometimes—this isn’t utopia—but you regain the ability to say:

> “No, let’s not do that. There’s a simpler pattern that achieves the same user experience.”

That’s not a full solution to web bloat, but it’s meaningful **leverage**.

---

## Designers Owning More of the Product

The exciting part is what this means for designers:

- They’re no longer limited to Figma and slide decks.
- They can **own the experience end-to-end**: behaviour, layout, performance, and flows.
- They can test ideas directly with working UI, not just clickable prototypes.

We’re already seeing:

- Designers who ship small features directly as Svelte components.
- Product designers who maintain htmx-enhanced template libraries.
- Teams where “handoff” is replaced by “pairing on the actual code”.

AI here acts like a **multiplier**:
- It fills in boilerplate.
- It fixes syntax.
- It offers patterns so designers don’t have to memorise every API.

The human still decides what the experience should feel like.

---

## The New Power Role

The designer–engineer hybrid isn’t a fantasy job description; it’s a natural consequence of:

- Tools that respect the web’s original simplicity (Svelte, htmx, HTML/CSS/JS).
- AI that makes translation from intent to code dramatically cheaper.
- Teams that value speed, coherence, and ownership over strict silo boundaries.

This role looks a lot like a modern, opinionated version of the old **webmaster**:

- Deep empathy for users and aesthetics.  
- Enough engineering skill (and AI leverage) to implement real features.  
- A strong sense of when complexity is necessary—and when it’s just noise.  

For people who enjoy both design and code, **these are incredibly good years to be working on the web**. The stack is powerful, the tools are mature, and the path from vision to reality has never been more direct.

And the best part? You don’t have to choose between being “a real engineer” or “just a designer” anymore. You can be the person who makes the thing—and that’s where the real leverage is.
